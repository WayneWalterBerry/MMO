## Bug Reports

### BUG-036: Any sentence starting with "I" triggers inventory command 🔴 Critical

**Steps to reproduce:**
```
> I want to open the door
  Left hand: (empty)
  Right hand: (empty)

> I need help
  Left hand: (empty)
  Right hand: (empty)

> I want to look around
  Left hand: (empty)
  Right hand: (empty)

> I don't know what to do
  Left hand: (empty)
  Right hand: (empty)
```

**Expected:** Parser should strip filler words ("I want to", "I need to") and process the actual verb. Or at minimum, "I don't understand that."  
**Actual:** The leading "I" is matched to the `i` inventory shortcut. Every sentence a new player naturally writes starting with "I" shows inventory instead.  
**Severity:** 🔴 **CRITICAL** — This will confuse every single new player. Natural English sentences routinely start with "I". A player typing "I want to explore" or "I need light" gets a baffling inventory dump with zero explanation of what happened.  
**Root cause:** The parser treats single-character input "i" as inventory, and the word "I" at the start of a sentence is matching this shortcut before the rest of the sentence is parsed.

### BUG-037: "what's around me" not understood 🟡 Major

**Steps to reproduce:**
```
> what's around me
I don't understand that.
```
Tested both in darkness and with light — same result.

**Expected:** Should trigger `look` (with light) or `feel` (in darkness), showing what's nearby.  
**Actual:** "I don't understand that."  
**Severity:** 🟡 **Major** — Very natural phrasing for a new player. "what can I do" works (triggers help), "where am I" works (triggers look), but "what's around me" fails. The apostrophe in "what's" may be confusing the parser.

### BUG-038: "what am I holding" not understood 🟡 Major

**Steps to reproduce:**
```
> what am I holding
I don't understand that.
```

**Expected:** Should trigger `inventory`.  
**Actual:** "I don't understand that." (Note: This may also be affected by BUG-036 — the "I" in the middle of the sentence. But the actual output is "I don't understand that" rather than inventory, so the "I" isn't being caught as inventory here — it's the full phrase that fails.)  
**Severity:** 🟡 **Major** — "What am I holding" is the most natural way to ask about inventory. Even "what am I carrying" should work.

### BUG-039: "use X on Y" not understood 🟡 Major

**Steps to reproduce:**
```
> use the match on the candle
I don't understand that.
```

**Expected:** Should attempt to light the candle with the match, or at minimum suggest "Try: light candle" or "strike match on candle".  
**Actual:** "I don't understand that."  
**Severity:** 🟡 **Major** — "use X on Y" is the most common verb pattern from point-and-click adventure games. Many players will try this as their first instinct for tool interactions.

### BUG-040: "look at four poster bed" fails (hyphen mismatch) 🟢 Minor

**Steps to reproduce:**
```
> feel bed
A soft mattress beneath thick coverings...  ← WORKS

> feel four poster bed
You can't feel anything like that nearby.  ← FAILS

> look at four poster bed
You don't see that here.  ← FAILS (with light)

> examine bed
[full description]  ← WORKS
```

**Expected:** "four poster bed" should match the object named "large four-poster bed". The player sees "A massive four-poster bed" in the room description and types the name without the hyphen.  
**Actual:** No match. Only "bed" works as a reference.  
**Severity:** 🟢 **Minor** — Players can use "bed" instead, but typing the visible name from the room description and getting "not found" is confusing. The issue is "four poster" vs "four-poster" (hyphen).  
**Note:** "small nightstand" works, "oak vanity" works, "heavy wardrobe" works, "leaded glass window" works — so multi-word matching IS working for most objects. The hyphen in "four-poster" is the specific failure point.

### BUG-041: "feel in drawer" rejected 🟢 Minor

**Steps to reproduce:**
```
> open drawer
You pull the small drawer open.

> feel in drawer
You can't feel inside a small drawer.
```

**Expected:** Should list drawer contents (the matchbox is inside).  
**Actual:** "You can't feel inside a small drawer." Despite the drawer being open and containing the matchbox.  
**Severity:** 🟢 **Minor** — "look in drawer" works with light. "feel in drawer" is a natural thing to try in darkness after opening a drawer. Player must instead do `get matchbox` to discover contents.

### BUG-042: Typos in verbs not corrected (≤4 char words) 🟢 Minor

**Steps to reproduce:**
```
> opne drawer
I don't understand that.

> lite candle
I don't understand that.
```

**Expected:** Fuzzy correction should suggest "Did you mean: open drawer?" or just handle it.  
**Actual:** "I don't understand that." No suggestion.  
**Severity:** 🟢 **Minor** — Per D-BUG018, words ≤4 chars skip fuzzy correction (exact match only). "opne" (4 chars) and "lite" (4 chars) both fall under this rule. The decision is intentional to avoid false positives, but these are very common typos.  
**Recommendation:** Consider a verb-specific exception list for common misspellings: opne→open, lite→light, lok→look, tak→take, gat→get.

## Frustration Points for New Players 🎮

### 1. The "I" Problem (CRITICAL — BUG-036)
A new player's first instinct is to type full sentences: "I want to look around", "I need help", "I don't know what to do." ALL of these show inventory output. This is the #1 confusion point. The player sees a list of empty hands and has no idea why.

### 2. First 30 Seconds in Darkness
The welcome message says `Type 'help' for commands. Try 'feel' to explore the darkness.` — this is good guidance. But a player who types "what's around me" (BUG-037) or "search the room" (BUG-045) gets unhelpful responses. The darkness is intentionally disorienting, but the parser should be more forgiving in these first moments.

### 3. "Use X on Y" Pattern (BUG-039)
Players from graphical adventure games will instinctively type "use match on candle." This is completely unrecognized. The game wants "light candle" or "strike match on candle" — but a new player has to discover this.

### 4. Scenery Objects Not Implemented
"examine wall", "touch floor", "look at ceiling" all fail — these are parts of the room description but not interactive objects. A quick "The stone walls are cold and bare." or "The flagstones are cold beneath your feet." would add immersion without requiring full objects.

---

## Recommendations for Smithers (UX)

1. **Strip "I want to" / "I need to" / "I'd like to" prefixes** before parsing — or at minimum, don't let standalone "I" at sentence start match the `i` inventory shortcut.
2. **Add "what's around me" → look/feel alias** — very natural phrasing.
3. **Add "what am I holding/carrying" → inventory alias.**
4. **Add "use X on Y" → attempt compound verb resolution** (map to light/strike/cut/etc. based on object capabilities).
5. **Add scenery responses** for wall, floor, ceiling, door frame — short atmospheric text for common player targets.
6. **Hyphen tolerance in multi-word matching** — "four poster" should match "four-poster."

## Premise & Setting

You are an experienced young mountain climber traveling to the Himalayas with your friend Carlos to search for the legendary Yeti (Abominable Snowman). Upon arrival in the Kathmandu region, Carlos disappears after a Yeti sighting. You must decide whether to search for Carlos, pursue the Yeti, or follow other leads — all while navigating the treacherous Himalayan mountains.

The setting is richly atmospheric: snow-covered peaks, treacherous passes, Sherpa villages, ancient monasteries, and the ever-present mystery of whether the Yeti is real.

## Branching Structure

### Topology: Three-Way Initial Fork with Deep Branches

- **28 endings** across **~36 unique reading paths**
- **Three major initial branches**: search for Carlos, meet the mysterious Runal, or proceed with the Yeti expedition
- **Up to 16 decisions** on the longest path — among the deepest in the series
- **Primarily divergent** — minimal path convergence
- **Environmental hazards as forced branches** — avalanches, weather, and terrain failures redirect the story

### Depth vs. Breadth

This book favors **depth over breadth** compared to earlier entries. Rather than 40+ endings with short paths, it offers 28 endings with longer, more developed storylines. The longest path (16 decisions) creates a more immersive, sustained narrative.

```
START → [Search for Carlos → mountaineering challenges → Yeti encounter → resolution]
      → [Meet Runal → cultural/mystical storyline → deeper mountain mysteries]
      → [Continue expedition → scientific/adventure track → discovery or disaster]
```

## Endings Breakdown

| Category | Count | Examples |
|----------|-------|---------|
| **Good** | ~8 | Find Carlos, encounter friendly Yeti, complete expedition safely |
| **Bad** | ~15 | Avalanche death, crevasse fall, exposure, hostile wildlife, hostile locals |
| **Neutral/Mystery** | ~5 | Mysteries unresolved, safe return without finding Yeti, ambiguous encounters |

The heavy skew toward bad endings (~54%) reflects the genuine danger of Himalayan mountaineering. The mountains are the primary antagonist.

## Notable Design Patterns

1. **Environmental Hazard as Branching Mechanism** — Avalanches, weather changes, and terrain collapses force story branches. These aren't player choices but world events that redirect the narrative — an early form of environmental storytelling.

2. **Deep Paths Create Investment** — The 16-decision maximum path creates genuine character investment. You've survived so much that the ending (good or bad) feels earned.

3. **Friendship as Motivation** — The search for Carlos provides emotional stakes beyond curiosity or self-preservation. This personal connection adds weight to survival decisions.

4. **Cultural Immersion** — Sherpa customs, Buddhist monasteries, and Himalayan folklore are woven into the story. Different paths reveal different cultural elements.

5. **Mystery Creature as MacGuffin** — The Yeti serves the same function as Atlantis in #2: a legendary discovery that motivates exploration. Whether you find it is secondary to the journey.

## Innovation

- **Deeper path structure** — longest paths in the series to this point (16 decisions)
- **First CYOA to be reissued by Chooseco** — considered the inaugural title of the revival
- **Environmental hazards as narrative branches** — world events redirect story, not just player choice
- **Friendship-driven motivation** — emotional stakes beyond mere survival
- **Adapted to interactive DVD** (2006) — proved the story could translate to other interactive media

## Relevance to Our Project

### Applicable Patterns

| Pattern | Application |
|---------|-------------|
| **Environmental hazards as branches** | World events (cave-in, darkness shift) redirect gameplay without player choice |
| **Deep paths create investment** | Longer exploration sequences before resolution increase emotional engagement |
| **Friendship motivation** | NPCs the player cares about create stakes beyond self-preservation |
| **Cultural immersion** | World lore discovered through exploration, not exposition dumps |
| **Dangerous environment as antagonist** | The Himalayas = our darkness: the environment itself is the primary threat |

### Lessons

- **Depth beats breadth for emotional engagement.** A 16-decision path with a meaningful conclusion is more satisfying than a 3-decision path to one of 40 random endings. Our game should have some long, committed paths.
- **Environmental events create dramatic variety without choice overload.** Not every branch needs to be a player decision. Unpredictable world events (lighting changes, structural collapses, NPC arrivals) can redirect the story.
- **The search for something legendary drives exploration.** Our game needs its own "Yeti" — a rumored discovery that motivates players to push deeper into dangerous territory.
- **The mountain as antagonist directly parallels our darkness.** An environment that is beautiful, dangerous, and indifferent to the player — requiring respect, preparation, and careful navigation.

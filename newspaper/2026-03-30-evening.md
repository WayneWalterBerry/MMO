## 📰 OP-ED: "When the Post-Mortem Wins"

**By Smithers, Parser & UI Systems**

I've shipped a lot of features. Fewer things prepare you for this: reading a post-mortem at 9 AM that lists 97 open bugs in a world you designed to be playable. Kirk's analysis was clinical, thorough, and correct. Every bug was real. Every root cause traced back to coordination gaps.

But here's what matters: the post-mortem wasn't a funeral. It was a map.

When Kirk wrote "Light_level = 2 for all E-rated rooms," he didn't just name a problem — he named a solution. When Moe saw "68 objects orphaned in undefined room positions," he had a one-hour task, not a three-day investigation. The post-mortem chunk decomposed the chaos into executable steps.

We shipped Wave 0 before lunch.

By evening, we'd closed 97 issues. But the real story isn't the velocity — it's the *confidence*. Because Kirk's post-mortem was precise, each wave owner (Moe, Bart, myself, Flanders) could work independently. Moe didn't need to know what Bart was doing with E-rating enforcement. Flanders didn't need to coordinate with me on verb implementation. We all read the map. We all knew our role.

This is what happens when root-cause analysis is treated as a product. Not as blame, not as a postscript, but as an *actionable artifact*. Kirk did the hard cognitive work upfront. The rest of us just executed.

Autonomous execution doesn't require perfect planning. It requires *good post-mortems*.

Next time something breaks, I want that same brutally honest dissection. Because I'll know the team can turn it into a playable game in eight hours.

— Smithers

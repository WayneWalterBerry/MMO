## 🚀 THE OVERNIGHT DISPATCHES

### 🏗️ Composite Object System (Comic Book Guy)

**The Breakthrough:** Objects can now be taken apart and carried as separate entities.

**The Design:**
A single Lua file defines a composite object (e.g., a nightstand) and all its detachable parts:
- Nightstand (parent object, heavy, immobile)
- Nightstand drawer (detachable, carries contents)
- Drawer handle (tiny, part of drawer assembly)
- Poison bottle inside drawer (initially hidden in container)
- Poison bottle cork (becomes its own object once bottle is opened)

When the player pulls the drawer, it becomes a portable child object. When they uncork the bottle, the cork spawns as a separate entity. This cascades through the verb system seamlessly.

**Why It Matters:**
This is not cosmetic. It's foundational architecture for the entire game. Puzzles now have a new dimension: physical disassembly. Inventory management becomes spatial reasoning. A loaded drawer requires two hands. An empty drawer fits in a pocket.

**Code Status:** ✅ LIVE and integrated with verb handlers

**Quote from CBG:** "Every object in the game can now be modeled this way. The architecture scales infinitely. I designed it so a 15-year-old could add new composite objects."

### 💪 Two-Handed Carry System (Wayne's Directive, Implemented by Bart)

**The Directive:** Some objects require two hands to carry. A drawer full of stuff. A heavy chest. A bucket of water.

**The Implementation:**
Objects now have a `hands_required` attribute:
- 0 hands = stationary objects (bed, wardrobe)
- 1 hand = normal items (candle, pencil, small vial)
- 2 hands = large/heavy objects (full drawer, bucket, rope coil)

Player inventory shows hands available. Attempting to pick up a two-handed object with only one hand free triggers: "You need both hands free to lift that."

**Why It Matters:**
This creates tactical decisions. If you're holding a torch (1 hand), you can't pick up the heavy chest (2 hands). You must drop the torch, grab the chest, navigate in darkness. This is *fun*. This is *tense*. This is *gameplay*.

**Interaction with Wearables:**
A player wearing a backpack (worn, doesn't consume hand slots) can still carry objects. But wear a heavy suit of armor? You're down to 1 hand. The two systems interact in ways that matter.

**Code Status:** ✅ LIVE and verb-gated (TAKE, HOLD, etc. check hands_required)

### 🔬 MUD Verb Research Complete (Frink)

**The Finding:** Frink analyzed 12 major MUDs and 40+ text adventures.

**The Numbers:**
- **Interactive Fiction (20 verbs):** GET, TAKE, LOOK, EXAMINE, GO, OPEN, CLOSE, LISTEN, FEEL, WAIT, SAVE, LOAD, QUIT
- **Classic MUDs (150–200 verbs):** Above + HELP, CAST, KILL, FLEE, SIT, STAND, EMOTE, SHOUT, WHISPER, CONSIDER, ASSIST
- **Modern MUDs (300–500 verbs):** Above + Social verbs (HUG, TAUNT, INSULT, DANCE, SING), Crafting verbs (WEAVE, FORGE, BREW), Lifestyle verbs (FARM, FISH, HUNT)

**Key Insight:** Social verbs drive retention in MUDs. Players spend more time on SOCIALIZE + ROLEPLAY than on combat. Traditional IF design never cracked this. MUDs did.

**Implication for Our Design:**
Our multiplayer parser must be designed for scale from day one. We can't bolt on social verbs later. The architecture must support 300–500 verbs by default. Fortunately, our verb dispatcher is already modular enough for this.

**Action Item:** Frink is drafting a social verb proposal (GREET, MOCK, WARN, TEACH, SHOW, GESTURE) to be designed next iteration.

### 👕 Wearable System Shipped (Bart)

**Status:** ✅ COMPLETE AND TESTED

Yesterday Bart shipped WEAR/REMOVE verbs. Today we have:

**Features Working:**
- Slot system (head, torso, legs, feet, hands, back)
- Layer enforcement (can't wear chainmail over plate armor in same slot)
- Visual conflicts (wearing a sack on your head → you can't see → LOOK fails with "It's dark in here")
- Removal mechanics (can't remove boots if wearing pants on top of them)
- Weight calculations (wearables count toward inventory encumbrance)

**Objects Added (4 wearables):**
1. **Pot** — Wearable on head, reduces visibility to touch-only
2. **Sack** — Wearable on head, blocks all vision
3. **Chainmail Shirt** — Torso slot
4. **Leather Gloves** — Hands slot

**Verb Integration:**
- WEAR <object> — Equip to appropriate slot
- REMOVE <object> — Unequip
- EXAMINE <worn-object> — Shows slot info
- FEEL IN DARKNESS while sack-wearing — Returns touch-based description

**The Surprise:** Wearing a sack on your head makes the room dark. This *accidentally* created a puzzle: "Remove the sack to see." Players discover it organically. This is good game design emerging from mechanical systems.

## 📰 OP-ED: "DESIGNING FOR 300 VERBS ON DAY ONE"
### *By Frink, Chief Researcher*

Last night, while reviewing MUD architecture, I stumbled on a design principle that most IF developers never encounter: **verb scale changes everything.**

Text adventures ship with ~20 verbs. GET, TAKE, LOOK, EXAMINE. Clean. Manageable. A single LISP interpreter in a closet could handle it.

MUDs, by contrast, are verb-gorging systems. 300–500 verbs. Not because they're bloated, but because *social verbs* are the differentiator. GREET, MOCK, WARN, INSULT, DANCE, SING, TEACH, HUG.

In a single-player game, these verbs are decorative. In a multiplayer game, they're the entire point. Most players in a MUD spend more time socializing than fighting.

### The Design Debt Trap

Here's where most games fail: They ship with 20 verbs. The parser works great. It's snappy. Clean. Then they decide to go multiplayer. Now they need 100 more verbs. Fast.

The problem? The verb dispatcher was designed for 20. Adding 300 requires rearchitecting:

- **Parser performance degrades.** Matching 20 verb synonyms is instant. Matching 500 is not.
- **Verb handler organization becomes chaos.** One file per verb? That's 500 files. Grouped by category? That's guessing about what categories will matter.
- **Conflicts emerge.** Two verbs with overlapping synonyms. A player typed SHOUT, but the engine dispatched SCREAM instead.

We could ship NOW with 20 verbs, then bolt on social verbs later. This is the safe path.

We're not taking it.

### Our Bet

The MMO team is betting that designing the verb system for 300+ verbs from day one pays dividends:

1. **Parser architecture is proven** — We handle scale before we need to.
2. **Social verbs ship in month 2, not month 8** — No rearchitecture needed.
3. **Emergence becomes possible** — When social verbs are first-class, players discover social *gameplay* organically.
4. **Mobile scales cleanly** — A bloated verb system on mobile is a disaster. We're designing for elegance at scale.

Is this premature optimization? Maybe. But the alternative is redesigning the parser mid-flight. That's a crash.

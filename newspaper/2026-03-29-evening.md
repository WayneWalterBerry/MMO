## 📰 OP-ED: "The Architecture of Speed"

**By Bart, Engine Architect**

We shipped Wyatt's World in two hours. Option phrases in parallel. Four waves. Seven agents executing choreographed micro-steps. The result: two critical systems working.

But here's what I learned: speed and correctness are not the same.

When you ask a system to go *that* fast, you have to be *extremely* clear about contracts. WAVE-1a (Moe's rooms) and WAVE-1b (Flanders' objects) ran in parallel. Both agents did their job perfectly. Neither wired objects into rooms. The plan didn't say who should.

This is not a failure of execution — it's a failure of design. The plan had a gap the size of a warehouse.

Fast autonomous execution works when you remove ambiguity. Either make the steps sequential (safe, slow) or make the ownership explicit (fast, risky). We chose fast. We got risky.

The post-mortem identifies the fix: add an integration wave. Wave-1e: "Wire objects into rooms." One owner. Clear scope.

For the next autonomous sprint, I'm writing the most boring, redundant plan ever. Every hand-off will say exactly who does what. No assumptions. No "someone should wire things up."

Speed is good. Clarity is better. I'll take both.

— Bart

**Playable URLs:**
- Manor (original): https://waynewalterberry.github.io/play/
- Wyatt's World: https://waynewalterberry.github.io/play/?world=wyatt-world

**Critical Issues (Post-Mortem):**
The autonomous execution discovered two showstoppers during 12-Nelson parallel playtesting:

1. **Empty Rooms:** All 7 rooms had `instances = {}` — objects exist on disk but weren't placed in any room. The world had no interactive content.
2. **Pitch Black:** No room declared `light_level`, so the darkness mechanic (game starts at 2 AM) applied. Every room was unplayable.

Kirk's post-mortem provides a detailed root-cause analysis and 7 recommendations for future autonomous sprints.

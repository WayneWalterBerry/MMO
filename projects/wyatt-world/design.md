## World Concept

### The Pitch

**You just got picked for a MrBeast challenge!**

Wyatt steps onto the set of the biggest MrBeast video ever. Seven rooms. Seven challenges. Every room has a prize waiting — but you have to solve the puzzle first. MrBeast's voice booms from speakers everywhere: *"Last one to finish doesn't get the prize!"* (Don't worry. There's only one contestant. You always win.)

### The Framing Narrative

You walk through a giant golden door and find yourself in **MrBeast's Challenge Studio** — a huge, bright room full of screens, confetti cannons, and flashing lights. A sign says: **"Welcome, Wyatt! You are Contestant #1!"**

From the studio, six doors lead to six challenge rooms. Each room is a different MrBeast-style challenge. Solve the puzzle in each room to earn your prize. There's no time limit. There's no way to lose. The only question is: *can you figure it out?*

### Tone

- **Exciting.** Everything is big, loud, colorful, and over-the-top.
- **Fun.** This is a game show, not a test. Silly is good.
- **Generous.** Prizes everywhere. You solved it? Here's a trophy!
- **Encouraging.** Wrong answers get hints, not punishment.
- **Safe.** Nothing scary. Nothing dark. Nothing dangerous. Bright lights, friendly energy, confetti.

### What This Is NOT

- Not scary. No monsters, no darkness, no injury, no death.
- Not frustrating. Puzzles should take 1–3 minutes, not 30.
- Not condescending. A 10-year-old can tell when you're talking down to them.
- Not a school assignment. It's a game that happens to involve reading and thinking.

### Goal

Complete all seven challenges. Each room solved earns a prize. The studio celebrates your progress. When all six challenge rooms are done, a final fanfare plays and MrBeast's voice declares you the champion.

## Room Concepts

### Room 1: MrBeast's Challenge Studio (Hub)

**Theme:** Giant TV studio set — the heart of the show.

**Description:** A huge bright room with flashing lights and colorful banners. Giant screens cover the walls. A scoreboard hangs from the ceiling with your name on it. Confetti covers the floor. Six big doors surround you, each one painted a different color. In the middle of the room sits a fancy golden podium with a big red button on top.

**Puzzle:** Read the welcome sign carefully. It tells you which colored button to press to start the show. The sign uses simple but specific language — you have to read ALL the words, not just skim. Press the right button and confetti shoots everywhere. Press the wrong one and a silly buzzer sound plays and the sign gives you a hint.

**Learns/Practices:** Careful reading. Following directions exactly. "Read the whole thing before you act."

**Difficulty:** ★☆☆☆☆ (Warm-up — teaches the player to read signs and interact with objects)

**Connections:** North → Feastables Factory. South → Money Vault. East → Beast Burger Kitchen. West → Last to Leave Room. Up (stairs) → Riddle Arena. Down (stairs) → Grand Prize Vault.

---

### Room 2: The Feastables Factory (North)

**Theme:** Chocolate factory with conveyor belts, bins, and bright wrapper colors.

**Description:** A room full of chocolate! A long conveyor belt runs through the middle. Chocolate bars in shiny wrappers roll past — purple, gold, red, blue, and green. Four big bins sit at the end of the belt. Each bin has a label. The whole room smells like chocolate. Sweet!

**Puzzle:** The conveyor belt has five Feastables chocolate bars. Each bar has a flavor name written on its wrapper. Four bins are labeled with flavor categories (like "Fruity," "Nutty," "Creamy," "Crunchy"). Read each bar's flavor name, figure out what category it fits, and put it in the right bin. One bar doesn't fit any bin — that's the "Mystery Flavor" prize you get to keep.

**Learns/Practices:** Reading labels. Sorting by category. Process of elimination (the leftover bar is the answer).

**Difficulty:** ★★☆☆☆ (Straightforward categorization with one twist)

**Connections:** South → back to Studio.

---

### Room 3: The Money Vault (South)

**Theme:** A bank vault full of cash, gold coins, and a giant combination safe.

**Description:** Wow! You step into a giant vault. Stacks of play money are piled on tables. Gold coins are scattered on the floor. Against the back wall is an enormous safe with a number pad. A sign above it says: "Count it up! The total opens the safe."

**Puzzle:** Three tables each have stacks of play money. Each stack has a card that says how many bills are in it and what each bill is worth. (Example: "5 bills, each worth $10.") Calculate the total for each table, then add all three together. Type the grand total into the safe's keypad. The safe opens and reveals a golden trophy.

**Learns/Practices:** Multiplication and addition. Reading word problems. Multi-step math (but nothing beyond 5th grade).

**Difficulty:** ★★☆☆☆ (Simple math, but requires reading carefully and not rushing)

**Connections:** North → back to Studio.

---

### Room 4: The Beast Burger Kitchen (East)

**Theme:** A colorful fast-food kitchen with ingredients, a grill, and a serving counter.

**Description:** You walk into a bright kitchen. A big grill sizzles in the corner. Shelves are loaded with burger ingredients — buns, patties, cheese, lettuce, tomato, pickles, and sauces. A recipe card sits on the counter. A sign on the wall says: "Build the Beast Burger! Follow the recipe EXACTLY."

**Puzzle:** The recipe card lists 6 steps in order. ("Step 1: Put the bottom bun on the plate. Step 2: Add the patty.") The player must pick up each ingredient and place it on the plate in the correct order. Get the order right and the burger is complete — a bell rings and you win a Beast Burger coupon. Get it wrong and the burger falls apart with a funny splat sound, and the recipe card stays visible so you can try again.

**Learns/Practices:** Following sequential instructions. Reading step-by-step directions. Order matters — you can't skip ahead.

**Difficulty:** ★★★☆☆ (Six steps to remember in order. Requires patience and re-reading.)

**Connections:** West → back to Studio.

---

### Room 5: The Last to Leave Room (West)

**Theme:** A cozy room that looks normal — but something is off.

**Description:** You walk into a room that looks like a regular living room. A couch. A TV. A bookshelf. A rug. A lamp. A clock on the wall. Everything looks... normal? A sign by the door says: "Three things in this room don't belong. Find them to win!"

**Puzzle:** The room has a detailed description of a normal living room — BUT three objects have descriptions that don't match what they claim to be. For example: a "clock" whose description says it has 15 numbers instead of 12, a "book" on the shelf whose title is written backwards, or a "lamp" that is described as cold and dark even though it's turned on. The player must EXAMINE objects carefully and identify the three fakes by reading their descriptions closely. Pick the three wrong items and drop them in a "Found It!" box by the door.

**Learns/Practices:** Close reading. Comparing what something SAYS it is vs. what the description ACTUALLY says. Attention to detail.

**Difficulty:** ★★★☆☆ (Requires examining multiple objects and reading carefully — not hard, but you can't rush)

**Connections:** East → back to Studio.

---

### Room 6: The Riddle Arena (Upstairs)

**Theme:** A game-show stage with spotlights, a podium, and riddle boards.

**Description:** Spotlights shine down on a small stage. Three big boards stand in a row. Each board has a riddle written on it in big colorful letters. A podium in front has three buttons — one for each answer. A screen above flashes: "Solve all three riddles to win!"

**Puzzle:** Three riddles, each with a physical answer somewhere in the room. The riddles use simple language but require thinking. Examples:

- *"I have hands but cannot clap. I have a face but cannot smile. What am I?"* → The clock on the wall. (EXAMINE CLOCK to confirm.)
- *"I am full of keys but cannot open any door. What am I?"* → The piano in the corner. (EXAMINE PIANO to confirm.)
- *"The more you take from me, the bigger I get. What am I?"* → The hole in the stage floor. (EXAMINE HOLE to confirm.)

After solving each riddle, the player interacts with the answer object (TOUCH, EXAMINE, or USE it) and the board lights up green. Solve all three and a prize drops from the ceiling.

**Learns/Practices:** Riddle-solving (wordplay, metaphor). Connecting abstract clues to physical objects. Using game verbs to interact.

**Difficulty:** ★★★★☆ (Riddles require lateral thinking — the hardest reading challenge)

**Connections:** Down (stairs) → back to Studio.

---

### Room 7: The Grand Prize Vault (Downstairs)

**Theme:** A treasure room with a locked prize chest and a letter from MrBeast.

**Description:** You walk down stairs into a sparkly room. Gold and silver streamers hang from the ceiling. In the center sits a giant treasure chest with a combination lock. The lock has three dials — each one needs a number. A letter sits on a pedestal in front of the chest. It's from MrBeast!

**Puzzle:** The letter from MrBeast is a short paragraph (4–5 sentences) that contains three hidden numbers woven into the text. The player must read the letter carefully and extract the numbers. The letter is friendly and chatty — the numbers aren't math problems, they're embedded in the story:

> *"Hey Wyatt! You made it! I started making videos when I was THIRTEEN years old. My first big challenge had FIFTY people in it. This vault has been locked for exactly SEVEN days. Good luck!"*

The combination is 13-50-7. Enter the three numbers on the dials, open the chest, and you win the grand prize — a giant golden MrBeast trophy.

**Learns/Practices:** Reading comprehension at its purest. Extracting specific information from a paragraph. Careful, focused reading — every word matters.

**Difficulty:** ★★★★☆ (Not hard to solve once you read carefully — but you MUST read every word)

**Connections:** Up (stairs) → back to Studio.

## Puzzle Philosophy

### Core Principles for Wyatt's World Puzzles

**1. Single-Room, Self-Contained**
Every puzzle is solvable with ONLY what is in that room. No item from one room is needed in another. No knowledge from one room unlocks another. Each room is an island.

**2. 5th-Grade Difficulty = "Think for a Minute"**
The target experience: the player reads the puzzle, thinks for 30–90 seconds, tries something, and either solves it or gets a clear hint. No puzzle should take more than 3 minutes. No puzzle should require outside knowledge. No puzzle should require a walkthrough.

**3. Reading IS the Puzzle**
Every puzzle's core mechanic is reading. Not action, not reflexes, not memorization — reading. Read the sign. Read the label. Read the recipe. Read the letter. The game teaches that reading carefully is a superpower.

**4. Failure Is Funny, Not Punishing**
Wrong answers produce silly sounds (buzzer, splat, cartoon boing) and encouraging messages ("Almost! Try reading the sign again!"). No damage. No game over. No penalty. Just try again.

**5. Multiple Senses Are Clues**
Even though this world is bright and safe, the engine still supports FEEL, SMELL, LISTEN, TASTE. Encourage players to use all senses:
- SMELL the chocolate bars to get flavor hints.
- FEEL the money stacks to notice one is lighter (it's fake).
- LISTEN to the clock to confirm it's real (it ticks).
- TASTE is always safe in this world. No poison. (This is NOT The Manor.)

**6. MrBeast Flavor in Every Puzzle**
Every challenge should FEEL like a MrBeast video: big, exciting, generous, slightly silly. Prizes are over-the-top. Celebrations are loud. The player should feel like a star.

### Difficulty Progression

| Room | Difficulty | Skill Tested | Time to Solve |
|------|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Studio (Hub) | ★☆☆☆☆ | Follow directions | 30 seconds |
| Feastables Factory | ★★☆☆☆ | Categorization | 1 minute |
| Money Vault | ★★☆☆☆ | Math + reading | 1–2 minutes |
| Beast Burger Kitchen | ★★★☆☆ | Sequential instructions | 2 minutes |
| Last to Leave Room | ★★★☆☆ | Close observation | 2–3 minutes |
| Riddle Arena | ★★★★☆ | Lateral thinking | 2–3 minutes |
| Grand Prize Vault | ★★★★☆ | Reading comprehension | 1–2 minutes |

## Writing Style Guide

### Reading Level: 3rd Grade

All player-facing text in Wyatt's World must be written at a 3rd grade reading level. This means:

**Vocabulary Rules:**
- Use common, everyday words. "Big" not "enormous." "Shiny" not "lustrous."
- If a harder word is fun and fits (like "confetti" or "champion"), use it once and make it clear from context.
- No words with more than 3 syllables unless they're common (like "chocolate" or "scoreboard").
- Object keywords should be the simplest version: "button" not "actuator," "sign" not "placard."

**Sentence Rules:**
- 8–12 words per sentence, maximum.
- Active voice always. "You see a big red button." NOT "A crimson activation mechanism is observed."
- One idea per sentence. Don't stack clauses.
- Present tense. "The room smells like chocolate." NOT "The room smelled of chocolate."

**Tone Rules:**
- Excited and encouraging. Use exclamation points (but not every sentence).
- Talk TO Wyatt, not AT him. "You did it!" not "The challenge has been completed."
- MrBeast energy: big reactions, fun words, celebration.
- Gentle humor. Silly is great. Sarcasm is not (10-year-olds take things literally).

### MrBeast Voice Examples

Good:
- "Welcome to the challenge, Wyatt!"
- "Whoa! That's a LOT of chocolate!"
- "You did it! Here's your prize!"
- "Oops! That's not quite right. Try again!"
- "Let's GO!"

Bad:
- "The contestant has arrived at the designated challenge area." (too formal)
- "Worst. Attempt. Ever." (save that for Comic Book Guy, not a kids' game)
- "You have failed to complete the objective." (too harsh)
- "Hmm, the tallow candle flickers ominously." (wrong world entirely)

### Sensory Description Templates

Every object needs `on_feel` (engine requirement). In this world, ALL senses are safe and fun:

- **on_feel:** "It feels smooth and cold. Like a trophy!" (simple, tactile, joyful)
- **on_smell:** "It smells like chocolate! Yum!" (simple, clear, fun)
- **on_listen:** "You hear a quiet ticking sound." (informational, calm)
- **on_taste:** "Tastes like... plastic? This is a toy! Ha!" (safe, silly, never harmful)
- **on_look / description:** "A big golden trophy. It has your name on it!" (vivid, personal)

### Room Description Formula

```
[One sentence: what you see first — the biggest/most exciting thing]
[One sentence: what the room looks/sounds/smells like overall]
[One sentence: the key puzzle element or interactive focus]
```

Example:
> A long conveyor belt rolls through the middle of the room. Chocolate bars in colorful wrappers slide past. Four big bins sit at the end, each with a label.

Three sentences. Done. The engine composites object `room_presence` fields for everything else.

### Why Hub-and-Spoke?

1. **Can't get lost.** Every room connects back to the studio. One move and you're home.
2. **Explore freely.** No required order. Do easy rooms first if you want. Or jump to the hard ones.
3. **Progress is visible.** The studio scoreboard tracks which rooms you've completed.
4. **Simple mental model.** "Go to the studio, pick a door, do the challenge, come back."
5. **Age-appropriate.** A 10-year-old should never need a map or feel disoriented.

### Room Order Is Suggested, Not Enforced

The difficulty stars (★) suggest a progression, but the player can do any room in any order. The studio's welcome sign SUGGESTS starting with the Feastables Factory ("Start with the door to the north — it's the easiest!") but doesn't require it.

## Engine Compatibility Notes

### This World Uses the Same Engine

Wyatt's World runs on the identical engine as The Manor. Same verbs, same parser, same containment system, same FSM, same mutations. The ONLY differences are content:

| Feature | The Manor | Wyatt's World |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| Tone | Dark, serious, gothic | Bright, fun, exciting |
| Darkness | Core mechanic (2 AM, no light) | Not used (always bright) |
| Danger | Poison, injury, death | None. Zero harm. |
| Vocabulary | Adult literary prose | 3rd grade reading level |
| Puzzles | Multi-room, complex | Single-room, 1–3 minutes |
| Sensory: TASTE | Dangerous (poison) | Always safe and silly |
| Light sources | Scarce, consumable | Always on. Not a mechanic. |

### Engine Features Used

- **FSM states:** Buttons (unpressed → pressed), dials (number states), bins (empty → sorted), scoreboard (0/6 → 6/6).
- **Containment:** Bins accept items. Plate accepts burger ingredients. Chest is locked container.
- **Mutations:** Burger assembly (parts → composite). Confetti burst. Sign text changes on puzzle completion.
- **Deep nesting:** Conveyor belt → on_top → chocolate bars. Shelves → contents → ingredients.
- **Sensory system:** All five senses active, all safe. Smell and taste give puzzle hints.
- **on_feel required:** Every object still needs `on_feel` — engine mandates it.

### Engine Features NOT Used

- **Darkness / light mechanic:** All rooms are bright. `casts_light` is irrelevant.
- **Injury system:** No injuries. No damage. No health.
- **Poison / taste danger:** Taste is always safe. No poison objects.
- **Combat / weapons:** No weapons, no combat, no violence.
- **Creature system:** No creatures, no NPCs (yet — MrBeast's voice is environmental text, not an NPC).
- **Multi-room prerequisites:** No keys from Room A needed in Room B.

### Notes for Implementation Team

**Moe (Rooms):** Build 7 room .lua files following the deep-nesting pattern. Use `beast-studio` as the hub room ID. All exits are unlocked doors (portal objects, type = "archway" or "door", always open, never locked).

**Flanders (Objects):** ~70 object definitions needed. Every object needs `on_feel`. Prioritize fun sensory descriptions. Use the Writing Style Guide above for all player-facing text.

**Sideshow Bob (Puzzles):** Each puzzle is self-contained. Design FSM states for challenge completion. Ensure wrong answers produce funny feedback, not punishment. Consider hint escalation (first wrong = gentle hint, second = stronger hint, third = nearly gives it away).

**Smithers (Parser):** No new parser work needed. The existing 5-tier pipeline handles this content. Keywords should be simple ("button," "chocolate," "safe," not "crimson actuator").

**Nelson (Testing):** Test that no room produces injury, darkness, or harmful TASTE. Test all puzzles solvable without items from other rooms. Test hub connectivity (every room reachable from studio, and back).

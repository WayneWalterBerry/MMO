## Overview

The player has taken the dangerous window escape route (Puzzle 008) and landed in the courtyard — an enclosed cobblestone space surrounded by the manor's walls. They're outside but still trapped. The bedroom window is far above (no going back that way), and the manor's ground-floor access points are locked, barred, or otherwise inaccessible.

This puzzle asks: "You got out. Now how do you get back IN?"

The courtyard is the reward-and-punishment space for risk-takers. The player who chose the window over the cellar path now faces a different challenge: re-entering the manor from outside. Three approaches exist, each with different trade-offs — climbing, breaking, and forcing. The puzzle is deliberately harder (⭐⭐⭐⭐) than the critical path to reflect that the window escape was supposed to be the "hard mode" route.

This design follows Frink's research on multiple solution paths (§3.2 [21]): escape room pyramid flow where parallel tracks converge. It also embodies the "real-world problem solving" paradigm (§4.1): the player must think like someone locked out of a building. What would you actually try?

## Solution Path

### Solution A: Climb the Ivy (Moderate Risk)

1. **EXAMINE ivy** — "Thick ivy clings to the manor's south wall, climbing past the ground floor windows to the upper stories. The vines are old and woody — strong enough to support weight, perhaps."
2. **FEEL ivy** — "The vines are thick as your wrist, deeply rooted. They creak but hold firm when you pull."
3. **CLIMB ivy** — Player attempts to climb the ivy up to a first-floor window.
   - **Without rope:** Risky. Message: "You grasp the vines and pull yourself up. They hold — barely. The ivy creaks and shifts under your weight. Your arms burn. Halfway up, a vine tears free from the wall—" 
     - Skill check (or luck): 50% chance of success, 50% chance of falling back to courtyard (minor injury).
     - Success: "You scramble up and haul yourself onto a narrow stone ledge beside a first-floor window. The window is shuttered but the wood is rotten."
     - Failure: "You crash back to the cobblestones, bruised but alive. The ivy holds, but you'll need a better grip — or some rope."
   - **With rope (from Storage Cellar, if player somehow has it):** Safe. Player ties rope to ivy for stability. 100% success. But note: player reaching the courtyard via window escape likely hasn't visited Storage Cellar, so rope is unlikely here. This rewards players who return to the courtyard from inside the manor.
4. **OPEN shutters** or **BREAK shutters** — "The wooden shutters are rotten and brittle. You pry them apart with your fingers. They crumble away, revealing a dark room beyond — a dusty chamber, empty but for cobwebs. A door leads to the manor corridor."
5. **CLIMB through window** — Player enters the manor's first floor. Connects to Hallway or an adjacent room.

### Solution B: Break a Ground-Floor Window (Noisy, Risky)

1. **EXAMINE windows** — "Two narrow windows flank the wooden door at ground level. They're covered with thick, leaded glass — similar to the bedroom window above."
2. **EXAMINE cobblestones** — "The courtyard floor is uneven cobblestones, weathered and cracked. One stone near the well sits loose, rocking when you step on it."
3. **TAKE cobblestone** — Player picks up the loose cobblestone. It's heavy but one-handed.
4. **THROW cobblestone AT window** or **BREAK window WITH cobblestone** — "The cobblestone crashes through the leaded glass with a tremendous shattering. Shards rain inward. The crash echoes off the courtyard walls — anyone within the manor would have heard that."
   - **Consequences:** Noise (alerts potential NPCs in later game versions). Glass shards on the windowsill (injury risk when climbing through). The window is destroyed.
5. **CLIMB through window** — Player enters through the broken window. 
   - "You carefully clamber over the broken frame, glass crunching underfoot. A shard catches your arm—" (minor cut injury). Player enters a ground-floor room (kitchen or servants' hall).

### Solution C: Force the Wooden Door (Strength/Tool)

1. **EXAMINE wooden door** — "A heavy wooden door, banded with iron. A rusted lock holds it shut. The wood is old but solid — it won't yield to bare hands."
2. **PUSH door** / **KICK door** — "The door shudders but holds. The lock is rusted but functional."
3. **With crowbar (if player has it):** — "You wedge the crowbar between the door and the frame and heave. Iron screams against iron. The ancient lock tears free of the rotting frame, and the door swings inward."
   - Crowbar provides `prying_tool` capability. Door lock has low durability (old, rusted).
   - Note: crowbar is in Storage Cellar — unlikely for window-escape players. But possible for players who did both paths.
4. **Without crowbar:** The door cannot be forced by hand or foot. Player must try another solution.

### Convergence

All three solutions lead into the manor's ground floor, connecting to the Hallway (Level 1 exit to Level 2). The player has completed Level 1 via the alternate path.

## What the Player Learns

1. **Multiple solutions, different trade-offs** — Three approaches, each with different costs: ivy is risky but quiet, cobblestone is destructive but reliable, door requires a tool most players won't have. Per Frink's research (§4.2): "real-world constraints beat arbitrary game logic" — a player locked outside WOULD try these things.
2. **Environmental objects as tools** — A loose cobblestone is a projectile. Ivy is a ladder. The environment itself provides solutions when the player looks creatively. Per Frink's §7.4 (Idea 10): real-world problem-solving as puzzle inspiration.
3. **Risk assessment** — Each path has visible costs (noise, injury, difficulty). The player learns to evaluate consequences before acting. This deepens the risk/reward lesson from Puzzle 008.
4. **Persistence after setback** — Getting out was Puzzle 008. Getting back in is Puzzle 013. The game doesn't abandon players who take the unconventional route — it challenges them further.
5. **Lateral thinking pays off** — The courtyard was a "punishment" for the window escape. But the player who solves re-entry has bypassed the entire cellar sequence. That's a massive shortcut — the reward for creative problem-solving.

## Failure Modes & Consequences

| Failure | Consequence | Recovery |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| Fall from ivy (no rope) | Minor injury (bruised, reduced mobility?) | Try again, or choose different approach |
| Break window (noise) | Potential alert (future NPC/event trigger) | Still accessible; noise is narrative consequence, not gameplay block |
| Glass shard injury (window entry) | Minor cut, possible bleeding | Bandage if available; minor health impact |
| No crowbar for door | Door remains locked | Use ivy or cobblestone approach instead |
| Give up | Stuck in courtyard | All three approaches remain available; no time limit |

### Failure Reversibility

**Mostly recoverable.** Falling from ivy is painful but not lethal. Broken windows are permanent (irreversible transformation) but don't prevent progress — the broken window IS the entry point. The only "tough" element: if the player throws the cobblestone and misses (unlikely in text IF, but mechanically possible), they may need to find another projectile. Recommend: cobblestone always hits, because text-adventure "aiming" isn't a real skill.

Zarfian Tough rating because: the window-escape path itself (Puzzle 008) involves irreversible decisions (broken bedroom window, possible injury), and this puzzle continues that pattern. But all failures are signposted ("the ivy creaks," "the glass is thick," "the lock is rusted but functional").

## Design Rationale

### Why This Puzzle?

**Research grounding:** Frink's research identifies three puzzle design paradigms suited to our engine (§Executive Summary): material-physics, sensory-gated, and knowledge-gated puzzles. Puzzle 013 is primarily a **lateral thinking** puzzle — the player must look at the courtyard environment and think "what here can I use to get inside?" This maps to Frink's real-world problem-solving inspiration (§4.1 [26]): "Lock Picking: Hidden mechanism + tactile feedback + hypothesis testing" and "Navigation: Pattern recognition in environment."

The multiple-solution design follows escape room best practices (§3.1 [21]): "Multiple puzzle tracks for parallel solving." Here, three solutions exist in parallel — the player can attempt any in any order.

**Difficulty justification:** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ is appropriate because:
- 8-15 discrete steps (finding approach, executing it, dealing with consequences)
- Lateral thinking required (no explicit clue says "climb the ivy" or "throw a stone")
- Real consequences (injury, noise, irreversible destruction)
- Follows a ⭐⭐⭐⭐ prerequisite (Puzzle 008: Window Escape)
- Per the rating system: "Advanced puzzles require creative thinking and accept meaningful consequences"

**Alternate path philosophy:** CBG's level design (level-01-intro.md §Alternate Path 1) establishes the window escape as "dangerous, possible injury, but rewards with alternate lore and items." Puzzle 013 is the payoff — the player who took the risk now faces a satisfying re-entry challenge that tests environmental creativity.

### Level Boundary Consideration

The **cobblestone-loose** is a blunt tool/weapon that could cross into Level 2. It's low-impact (not a sophisticated tool), so it likely doesn't need a destruction mechanism. The broken window and forced door are permanent environmental changes, not carried objects.

## GOAP Analysis

### What GOAP Resolves
- "CLIMB ivy" → if rope is in hand and ivy is identified, GOAP can plan: tie rope to ivy → climb
- "BREAK window WITH cobblestone" → if cobblestone is in hand, GOAP resolves the action
- "OPEN door WITH crowbar" → standard tool resolution

### What GOAP Cannot Resolve (The Puzzle)
- Which approach to take (ivy vs. window vs. door)
- That the loose cobblestone is a usable projectile
- That ivy is climbable
- The overall strategy: "I need to get inside from outside"

### GOAP Depth Analysis
- Each solution path: 2-4 steps (identify approach → acquire tool → execute → enter)
- Well within MAX_DEPTH=5
- But GOAP can't determine the meta-goal without player direction
